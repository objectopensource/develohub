A Guide to Better Online Privacy

Cookies and analytics are eveywhere on the web today. You're being spied on by companies like Google and Facebook. But with a few little steps, you can be more private wherever you go one the Internet.

Why do you need to care?

You're being followed around by trackers, and they're almost completely for advertising. And who clicks on ads? Lesser than 10% of all people who see them.
Let's say you were searching for a new phone on a few sites which all use Google for their tracking. Then, in pretty much every page that uses Google Ads, you'll see only phone advertisements. That's how these cross-site tracking systems try to get you to click on ads. Google makes a lot of money out of these ads (lots of information about Google's advertising profits is available everywhere). Companies sell your data to advertisers, which no one really likes.

1. Web browser

The simplest way to have better overall privacy is to make sure your browser is private.
Google Chrome is not a private browser (Google is the biggest tracking company on the Internet, so don't expect them to make Chrome private). Consider switching to a private web browser from this list (order from most private to least private):

  • Brave Browser
  • Vivaldi Browser
  • Mozilla Firefox
  • Apple Safari

Which should you choose?

If you want maximum privacy, try Brave. The most important feature about Brave is that it has built-in ad and tracker blocking. Since it's based on Chromium, it will look and feel like Chrome. It's also the fastest browser in this list!

Vivaldi is the most customisable option, and is almost as private as Brave, since it offers built-in tracker and ad blocking too. Vivaldi is also based on Chromium. The speed is around the same as Brave's speed as well. Vivaldi is the most feature-rich and customisable option in this list, but it does take a little bit longer to launch compared to the other browsers.

The oldest browser on the list is Firefox. Out of all the browsers on the list, Firefox is the one with the best ecosystem. It supports all major operating systems on desktop and mobile, and has a great syncing feature which encrypts your passwords while syncing.With a Firefox account, you can do more than just syncing. You can use Pocket, Firefox Monitor which alerts you if your data appeared in a breach, and more coming soon. Firefox blocks lots of trackers by default too.

If you only use Apple products, you can stick with Safari. It's private and simple and has all the basic features anyone would need.

Settings to change in any browser

Look for a setting in your browser which says something like "help improve this browser" or "send usage data" and turn it off. It makes things just a bit more private although no one is making money with this data or selling it.

2. Search Engine

For a bigger step towards online anonymity, switch from Google Search to DuckDuckGo or Startpage. DuckDuckGo has great instant answers and Startpage gets its results from Google without tracking you. Both these search engines don't track your searches or location. DuckDuckGo has a "Bangs" feature, which lets you search lots of other websites through DuckDuckGo itself. For example, if you type "!gh duckduckgo", it'll take you to the GitHub search page and searches for "duckduckgo." Startpage also has a special feature called "Anonymous View", which opens the result within the Startpage site, which completely masks your identity.

3. An Optional Browser Extension

There are a few browser extensions that can improve your privacy. If you're using Firefox, you can install uBlock Origin which is an ad and tracker blocker. Since Brave and Vivaldi already have good built-in blocking capabilities, you don't need it (but you could install it on Vivaldi if you want to, since Vivaldi doesn't block as many trackers as Brave)
